# Ozempic (胰妥讚 / 诺和泰) Knowledge Base

> Note: This document summarises the approved uses, dose forms, and safety profile of Ozempic. **It is reference material only, not medical advice.** Any decision involving dose adjustments, injection technique, storage, or side-effect management should be confirmed with a prescribing physician or pharmacist.

Sources: FDA Prescribing Information (DailyMed), EMA Product Information, Novo Nordisk manufacturer resources, SUSTAIN clinical trial program, r/Ozempic community pattern summaries.

---

## Core facts

- **Manufacturer**: Novo Nordisk
- **FDA approval**: 2017 (type 2 diabetes glycemic control); subsequent expanded indications for cardiovascular risk reduction and chronic kidney disease
- **EMA approval**: 2018 (adult type 2 diabetes glycemic control; MACE and CKD indications added)
- **TFDA (Taiwan)**: Approved; product name 胰妥讚 injection, indicated for type 2 diabetes (license 衛部菌疫輸字第 001107 號, listed 2019-06-24)
- **Generic name**: semaglutide (司美格魯肽 / 司美格鲁肽), GLP-1 receptor agonist
- **Approved indications**: Glycemic control adjunct in adults with type 2 diabetes (primary indication); reduction of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) in patients with T2D and established cardiovascular disease; reduction of progression of chronic kidney disease in adults with T2D and CKD. **Weight management is not an approved indication** (off-label use occurs; Wegovy is the branded weight-management formulation).
- **Form**: Pre-filled multi-dose pens (red 0.25/0.5 mg, blue 1 mg, yellow 2 mg)
- **Standard titration**: 0.25 mg once weekly for 4 weeks (initiation only, not glycemically effective); increase to 0.5 mg at week 5; may increase to 1 mg after ≥4 weeks on 0.5 mg; maximum 2 mg once weekly
- **Dosing frequency**: Once weekly, subcutaneous injection (皮下注射)
- **Half-life**: Approximately 7 days; ~5 weeks to clear from circulation after discontinuation
- **Storage**: Unopened: 2–8°C (refrigerate; do not freeze). After first use: 15–30°C or refrigerated, maximum **56 days (US Prescribing Information)**; EMA allows **6–8 weeks depending on pen configuration**. Protect from light.

## Common community misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "Ozempic is a weight-loss drug"

**Incorrect.** Ozempic is FDA-approved exclusively for type 2 diabetes glycemic control and cardiovascular risk reduction in specific populations. Weight loss is a documented side effect, not the primary indication. Off-label weight-management use occurs but is not manufacturer-supported. **Wegovy** is the same active ingredient (semaglutide) but is specifically labelled, dosed, and approved for chronic weight management. The two are not interchangeable for weight loss despite using the same molecule.

### Misconception 2: "Ozempic is insulin"

**Incorrect.** Ozempic is a glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonist. It works by mimicking the incretin hormone GLP-1, which stimulates insulin secretion *only* in the presence of elevated blood glucose. This glucose-dependent mechanism contrasts with insulin, which lowers blood sugar regardless of glucose level, and carries lower hypoglycemia risk when used alone. Insulin and Ozempic are separate drug classes with different mechanisms.

### Misconception 3: "GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) are permanent"

**Incorrect.** Gastrointestinal side effects—including nausea (噁心 / 恶心), diarrhea (腹瀉 / 腹泻), vomiting (嘔吐 / 呕吐), and constipation (便秘)—are most pronounced during the titration phase. EMA and FDA label data show these effects are usually mild to moderate and tend to resolve or improve as the body adjusts. Some users report persistent mild symptoms, but the majority experience significant improvement by 6–12 weeks of treatment. Slower titration can reduce symptom severity.

## Use and storage essentials

- **Injection sites**: Subcutaneous injection (皮下注射) into abdomen, thigh, or upper arm. Rotate injection sites to reduce risk of lipohypertrophy (local fat buildup).
- **New needle every injection**: Use a new sterile needle for each dose; needles are single-use.
- **Timing between doses**: At least 48 hours between doses (US Prescribing Information); EMA recommends ≥72 hours. Both allow a dose to be shifted to a different day of the week if needed.
- **Missed dose protocol**:
  - If ≤5 days late: inject as soon as you remember.
  - If >5 days late: skip that dose and resume your regular schedule the next week.
  - Do not double-dose to make up.
- **Storage after opening**: 15–30°C or refrigerated; do not freeze. **Maximum 56 days at room temperature (US); EMA allows 6–8 weeks depending on pen type.**
- **Inspection**: Solution should be clear and colorless. Do not use if cloudy, discoloured, or contains particles.

## Common side effects

### Very common (>20% in SUSTAIN trials)
- **Nausea (噁心 / 恶心)**: Usually mild–moderate; onset within first 1–2 weeks; improves by week 8–12 in most users. If persistent, notify prescriber.
- **Diarrhea (腹瀉 / 腹泻)**: Ranges from loose stools to more frequent bowel movements. Hydration, dietary fibre adjustment may help.

### Common (5–20%)
- **Vomiting (嘔吐 / 呕吐)**: Less frequent than nausea; typically resolves with continued treatment.
- **Constipation (便秘)**: Paradoxically reported alongside diarrhea; varies by individual.
- **Abdominal pain / distension**: Usually mild; resolves with time.
- **Decreased appetite (食慾不振 / 食欲不振)**: Intentional mechanism; may contribute to weight loss in off-label contexts.
- **Fatigue (疲勞 / 疲乏)**: Reported in some users; usually transient.
- **Injection site reactions**: Redness, bruising, mild swelling at injection site; usually resolves in a few hours.
- **Headache**: Mild, infrequent.

### Uncommon/Serious (require prompt medical evaluation)
- **Acute pancreatitis**: Rare but serious; presents as severe upper abdominal pain, back pain, nausea. Seek emergency care immediately.
- **Gallbladder events** (biliary colic, cholecystitis): Increased risk reported in weight-loss contexts. Report right-sided abdominal pain or jaundice.
- **Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)**: Risk increases if used with insulin or sulfonylureas (SU); monitor blood glucose.
- **Acute kidney injury (AKI)**: Reported in context of severe dehydration from vomiting/diarrhea. Maintain adequate hydration; report oliguria or elevated creatinine.
- **Retinopathy complications**: Diabetic retinopathy may worsen transiently in the early weeks of treatment, especially if blood glucose drops rapidly. Baseline ophthalmology assessment recommended if history of diabetic eye disease.
- **Allergic reactions**: Rash, hives, urticaria; rare anaphylaxis. Discontinue and seek medical care if signs appear.

### **Boxed warning (most serious)**: 
- **Thyroid C-cell tumors (medullary thyroid carcinoma, MTC)**: Animal studies showed dose-related MTC risk. Contraindicated in personal or family history of MTC or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) syndrome type 2.

## Drug interactions and contraindications

### Contraindications
- Personal or family history of MTC or MEN 2
- Known hypersensitivity to semaglutide or any component of the formulation

### Major interactions
- **Insulin or sulfonylureas**: Concurrent use increases hypoglycemia risk. Dose reduction of insulin/SU may be necessary when starting or escalating semaglutide. Monitor blood glucose closely.
- **Warfarin / acenocoumarol (coumarins)**: No major INR change in formal studies, but **decreased INR has been reported with acenocoumarol**. Frequent INR monitoring recommended when initiating semaglutide; dose adjustments of anticoagulant may be needed.
- **Oral medications**: Delayed gastric emptying may affect absorption of some oral drugs, particularly those requiring rapid gastric absorption. Spacing dosing and monitoring therapeutic levels of sensitive medications (e.g. birth control, antibiotics) advised.
- **Alcohol**: No formal contraindication, but may increase hypoglycemia risk, especially if combined with insulin or SU.

## Residual medication (multi-dose pens)

### The facts
- The Ozempic pen is designed to deliver 4 doses; a small amount of fluid remains after the fourth dose.
- **Manufacturer's explicit instruction**: Do not attempt to extract or use leftover medication.
- Community discussions (r/Ozempic, "golden dose" / "residual dose" threads) describe off-label extraction attempts using insulin syringes. **Any extraction departs from manufacturer instructions and introduces risks.**

### Why extraction is risky
1. **Sterility loss**: Puncturing the pen housing multiple times compromises sterility; infection and abscess risk is real.
2. **Dosing inaccuracy**: Without the pen's mechanical dosing system, the actual dose delivered is unpredictable. Residual fluid concentration may vary.
3. **Shelf-life uncertainty**: The 56-day storage window applies to the four-dose regimen under manufacturer conditions; extracted residual has no validated shelf life.

### What only a clinician should decide
- Whether to consider residual use at all in individual cases.
- Any specific procedure or dose adjustment.

An informational document should not include step-by-step extraction instructions; that is a medical procedure requiring direct physician or pharmacist oversight.

## Lifestyle considerations during treatment

- **Hydration**: Maintain adequate fluid intake, especially during the first weeks when GI symptoms are common. Dehydration increases AKI risk.
- **Protein intake**: Protein is important to preserve lean muscle mass during weight loss (off-label context). Nutrition consultation recommended if using off-label for weight management.
- **Alcohol**: Caution advised; may increase hypoglycemia risk if combined with insulin or sulfonylureas.
- **Pregnancy**: Semaglutide is not studied in pregnancy and is contraindicated. Discontinue ≥2 months before attempting conception. If pregnancy is confirmed during treatment, consult prescriber immediately.
- **Fertility**: Limited data on effects on fertility. Discuss family-planning intentions with prescriber before starting.
- **Going on/off**: Do not stop abruptly without medical guidance. Some prescribers recommend gradual de-escalation. If restarting after a gap, re-titration from 0.25 mg is recommended.
- **Common OTC interactions**: Most OTC medications are compatible, but antacids may affect absorption of some compounds. Consult pharmacist if on long-term antacid therapy.
- **Concurrent GI medications**: If using GLP-1 together with metformin, acarbose, or other GI-active agents, monitor for compounded GI effects and discuss with prescriber.
